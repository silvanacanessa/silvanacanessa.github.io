The Four Humours

Hippocratic medicine, based on the theory of the four bodily humours, assumed that women’s humours were cool and wet, as opposed to men’s, which were hot and dry.

Therefore, women required sexual intercourse to heat and drain the blood, which is what led to women’s insatiable desire for semen.

This early medical theory, though archaic by modern standards, laid the foundation for enduring assumptions about female sexuality that persist to this day.

The Malleus Maleficarum (Witch’s Hammer), c. 1486, was the handbook on witchcraft and heavily influenced to ignite and sustain the witch-hunting hysteria in Europe.

It explained that women, due to their insatiable lust, were more prone to witchcraft, and authors suggested that women’s moral and mental weakness was the origin of this deviance.

Season of

the Witch

Women were deemed as succubi, acting as servants and slaves to the devil, while the devil assumed the role of the owner and master. Men were able to easily accuse women of bewitching them, particularly if they were caught in any form of perceived deviant or perverse sexual acts.

Witches were said to be able to instill an unshakeable erotic passion in men due to their supposed demonic abilities.

"Women… are lovely to look at, but contaminating to touch; they attract men, but only to undermine them; they do everything to please them, but the pleasure they give, is more bitter than death, for their vices cost men, the loss of their souls - and perhaps their sexual organs"

Malleus Maleficarum

A New Kind of Witch

As the time of witch accusations diminished in relevance, a new allegation emerged: “nymphomaniac”.

Despite the change in terminology, the underlying accusation stayed the same, an overly sexual woman harbouring hidden hostility and believed to be able to diminish a man’s sexual vigor.

In 1775 was the first comprehensive study of nymphomania as a medical diagnosis, attributing strong sexual desire in women as a symptom.

Nymphomania Treatments

Treatment for the supposed disease was harsh and often invasive, including...

Vaginal swabbing with borax solution
Ice application to the genitals
Leeches applied to the uterus
Vaginal douching

Male physicians were warned about manipulative patients who acted modestly but actually sought genital inspections for their sexual gratification during medical visits.

“They are tempters of the opposite sex, purveyors of disease, and spreaders of vicious knowledge among other girls”

Freudian theorists compared the insatiable sexual needs of a nymphomaniac to an infant’s perpetual hunger, a desire that was never entirely fulfilled. Influential gynecologist, Bernard S. Talmey, furthered this narrative by asserting that “the weakest, most delicate woman is able to tire out the strongest man”.

Weak and

Weird Women

The characterization of nymphomaniacs as dangerous, unnatural, and sexually uncontrollable extended to any woman engaging in sexual activity outside of societal norms. This included lesbians, prostitutes, feminists, and working women, all of whom were accused of harbouring the same supposed disease and deemed dangerous.

Witch on the Web

We often assume that accusations like “witch” or diagnoses like “nymphomaniac” belong to the past, things we’ve outgrown. But have we?

Modern social media platforms are filled with discussions that echo the same anxieties: from policing women’s sexual agency to branding them as either “pure” or “corrupt”. These ideas haven’t disappeared, they’ve just adapted to new mediums.
